What are the various types of body parts?

The Yoruba have appropriate descriptions for the body parts. As with any other group of people, the different parts of the body are variously and indigenously labeled. Some of these body parts labels include: ojú (eye) that is used for sight (fún ríríran); etí (ear) that is used for hearing (fun gbígbóràn); enu (mouth) that is used for tasting (fún títówò) and speaking (fún sísòrò); imú (nose) that is used for smelling (fún gbígbóòórùn); owó (hand) for touching (fún fífowókan nnkan) and so on. The body can be divided into four segments of the head (orí) including the neck (orùn); the torso (ara); the arm (apá) from the shoulder (èjìká) to the fingers (ìka owó); and the legs (esè) from the thighs (itan) to the foot (atelese). The private parts and the left hand are the only body parts that are circuitously labeled in Yoruba language. The vagina is known as ojú-ara (the eye of the body) and penis is called nnkan omokùnrin (the man’s thing/tool). Despite the fact that Yoruba acknowledged the role played by the left hand (owó òsì) with the proverb ‘fi otun we osi fi osi we otun ni owo fi n mo” (that is, hands become clean if you use both hands to wash each other), they often times say that the left hand is less valuable, especially since it is the hand you use to clean up in the restroom. They even consider it an insult if someone offers them something with a left hand. All the parts of the body are believed to serve significant and mostly indispensable functions. The Yoruba therefore consider anyone that is deformed (slightly or considerably) as aláàbò ara. Interestingly, the body parts also form the basis for the derivation of various grammatical and idiomatic expressions in the Yoruba language. For example, prepositional materials are derived from the combination of ní (at) with a body part as in lórí (on that is derived from ni + orí at head) and léyìn (behind that is derived from ní + èyìn at back).

 

 

How are people described in Yoruba Land?

A unique component of communication is the strong element of forthrightness with which the physical description of different people is presented. People are described in relation to their physical appearance whether they are tall or short, fat or slim. One may be fat (sanra) or slim (tín-ín-rín) or tall (ga) or short (kúrú). In addition to making use of these descriptive terms candidly, the Yoruba language also appellations that are used to refer to people with some of these physical attributes. For example, a tall, thin or lanky individual might be given the nickname, Opelenge or Alonge while a fairly fat or big  person might be referred to as Orobo. Interestingly, the standard on beauty, especially for females, among the Yoruba people is different from many other parts of the world. Whereas, being skinny or slim is considered the beauty standard in the West, being relatively big or being judged ‘plump’ is the Yoruba standard. In actuality, being plump, especially in the case of Yoruba men, is an indication of affluence or wealth while being relatively thin or slim might be construed to mean that the person is impoverished or ‘living from hand to mouth.’
