Personal Schedule and the periods in the day (and in a person’s life)

The Yoruba people place great value on personal responsibility within the community. The day is divided into parts which helps the Yoruba people, even in traditional societies, to structure their daily existence. It is also believed that there is a good time suited for every activity and so, each Yoruba person is expected to manifest individual responsibility by planning their day and lives in such a way to maximize productivity. As they are wont to say, ‘òwúrò lójó’ meaning that the morning has great value and would be best time to work hard. Likewise, they commonly hold that a responsible schedule must include time for relaxation. The day is divided into parts which helps the Yoruba people, even in traditional societies, to structure their daily existence. There are several parts to the day. Idaji or afejumo is the dawn; owuro or aaro is morning; iyaleta is mid day; osan is the afternoon; irole is evening/dusk which, traditionally, marks the end of the work day; ale is night time following dusk; and oru is middle of the night when everyone is presumed sleeping. The Yoruba associate time in the life of an individual with developmental growth corresponding to the progression of time in a day. The Yoruba believe that one must ‘wake-up’ in life and do things that must be done at the appropriate time. For instance, when an individual is old enough to own a farmland, he must be given one by his father or by the Olori-ebi (Family head). When he/she is old enough to get marry just like his peers, he/she must not tarry or else, people around him will call him to a meeting and tell him/her in a subtle way through proverbs and wise saying. Particularly, they believe that one must work hard when one is young and that in one’s old age or ojo ale, one must sit down to enjoy all that one has labored for in life. Anybody, for whatsoever reason, still works when he/she is old and in the ale of their life is not favorably viewed in the society at all.

 

Personal Cleanliness 

The Yoruba abhor dirty environment. They place high premium on their personal hygiene and even take absolute pride in living a healthy life. This is reflected in the personal names they bear. Some of these names include marindoti “don’t walk around dirty,” ajisefinni “he who wakes up to look very neat.” Personal hygiene for the Yoruba begins with dental hygiene. They have chewing sticks of various kinds that they use in cleaning their teeth. These chewing sticks or orin are roots or branches considered antiseptic and medicinal. These render the teeth white, the mouth odorless and free of germs. Women deliberately create gap teeth by filing a portion of the two front teeth. These gap teeth make them to look pretty. Bathing, at least once a day is a must. During the dry season, when the heat is high, they can bathe two to three times a day. They use different local lotions to rub their body. Some of these include; epo gbigba or heated palm oil; epo pupa or red oil; osun or camwood ointment; adi agbon or palm kernel oil and ororo or vegetable oil, just to mention a few. They also rub their hands and the outer parts of their legs with laali. It is customary for women to cut their nails, both hands and legs regularly, while men leave theirs to grow, but they still maintain it regularly. Women and older men put on tiro or stibiun to their eyelashes. They also add powder (which is grinded from a type of stone) to their faces after bathing. As men go about with their heads cleanly and neatly shaven every time, the reverse is the case with women who ‘consider their hair as the glory of the woman.’ They take care of their hair in two major ways. They plait and they weave. There are many types of plaiting that women readily pick any type they want. Some of these include kolese, ipako-elede, suku, kojusoko, alagogo, konkoso etc.
