How is friendship viewed among the Yoruba?

Yoruba take friendship serious. Friends may be of the same age grade or one of them may even be older than the other. A friend is known as ‘òré.’ To the Yoruba people, general acquaintances or ‘àmòrí’ are many but it is only an ‘òré’ that will constantly provide support. There are therefore many levels of friendship: ‘òré tòótó’ is the true friend that provides support during the difficult times; ‘òré tímótímó’ is the very close friend; and ‘òré kòríkòsún’ is the ‘inseparable’ best friend.’ These however do not represent any actual scale of closeness but rather it identifies how one can perceive different friends and different friendships. It is possible for one person to be(come) all these to another person. In actuality, Yoruba people are of the opinion that some friends are or do become closer than blood relations. Those who are so close do almost everything in common ranging from building of the same type of structure, wearing the same type of attire when going for any function to having their farmland situated together. Some may even go to the extent of allowing their children to marry each other with the belief that it will further strengthen their friendship.

What are the implications for other relationships?

It is also a known fact that close friendships have been the basis for the establishment of many great organizations and businesses. Popular belief has it that such unions, ‘òré degbé’ group borne of friendship, are the best and most durable social grouping. It is actually the ‘friendship’ spirit that sustains the extended family within Yoruba societies. As a unit consisting of many nuclear families, the various members of the family are primarily expected to respect their elders but they are also required to be cordial in their dealings with age mates and younger people in the family. Outside the family, a significant level of cordiality is also expected amongst people who share similar trades or in modern times, are co-workers.
