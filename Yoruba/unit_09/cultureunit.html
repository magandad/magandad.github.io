Who does the cooking and what is involved?

Traditionally, the female members of the Yorùbá society are considered the culinary experts and manager of the family cuisine. This major role is in addition of the major contributions that they make in the agricultural sector. Things are changing nowadays with both men and women getting involved in food preparation. Children, particularly girls, are expected to be fully involved in the food preparation process, from helping to prepare the ingredients such as grinding pepper or grains to the actual cooking. The cooking areas in traditional compounds are mostly located in the rear or side of the house. These are often physical structures constructed to allow for adequate ventilation and storage. Traditionally, Yorùbá people were not known for cooking within their living quarters but that is also changing in the contemporary Yorùbá society. In addition to providing assistance to the cooks, children learn how to cook by observing their mothers and seniors as they cook. This is interesting because this practical method makes food preparation experiential allowing the young women to learn their mistakes and successes. Uniquely, Yorùbá people do not include definite measurements in their recipes and so the amount of ingredients for preparing each meal will based on practical factors such as the taste and experience of the cook and the amount of food to be prepared or the number of guests. This imprecise measurement of ingredients even applies in today’s society including the preparation of non-traditional meals. 

 

What are the main types of food?

Numerous foods abound in various parts of Yorùbá land. Other types of food are also in the land due to the interaction of the Yorùbá people with other ethnic groups or nationalities. These include yam (boiled, fried or roasted, yam flour, and meals made from yam such as ikokore, asaro, dundu), cassava (and its various derivatives such as gaari and lafun), bean (and its various derivatives like akara, or bean cake, moin-moin, and cooked beans), and corn (roasted or boiled and its various derivatives like ogi, eko) among others. While some of these foods are common to the generality of the Yorùbá people, there are some that are more localized in certain parts of the land. For instance, although pounded yam can be found in almost all parts of Yorùbá land, the Ekiti, Ijesa, Ife and Akure are fond of it. The Oyo are fond of prepared yam flour more than any other group. The Egba are fond of cassava flour more than any other group while the Ijebu are fond of ikokore and gaari. Rice and its various derivatives can also be found in all parts of Yorùbá land. We are not too certain if it is indigenous to the people or if it is introduced to them by Europeans or nations from the Sahel. We are, however, sure that bread is introduced to them by the Europeans. Different varieties of food can also be made from corn. Some of these include, ogi, eko, Kengbe / Adalu, tuwo; gúgúrú etc.

 

How are these food prepared and shared?

One thing that is common to yam, cassava, and plantain is that they can be processed and ground into flours. The flours can then be kept in cool and dry places. Whenever one is ready to eat it, very hot water is needed. Certain quantity is poured into the boiled water. It is stirred and mixed together for a while until it is firm. The food is then served into plates. There are various types of stew and soup that can be used in eating the prepared food. Vegetable soup is common among the Yorùbá. Stew is equally prepared in separate pot with meat, fish, or poultry. The two are then added together in a plate and served with pounded yam, cassava flour or even plantain flour. This meal combination highlights Yoruba traditional cuisine. Interestingly, this combination (meal, stew and meat/fish) is the pre-eminent lunch in contemporary Yoruba society. Since Yoruba live a communal life. The entire extended family, in most cases, eats together in a single plate. The food is served into a big bowl and everybody sit round it. Each one dips his hand into the dish and takes a morsel in strict order of seniority. By the time the food is diminishing in the bowl, care is taken to leave part some portion for the youngest.
