Yorùbá numbering system

The Yorùbá numeral system is intricate and unique. Majority of the numerals within the system are derived. In actuality, with the exception of one to ten and isolated numbers such as twenty ogun; thirty ogbon; two hundred igba; three hundred oodunrun; and four hundred irinwo, all others are derived. Excluding thirty, three hundred and four hundred, these exceptions in fact serve as the Åebuilding blocksÅf for the formation of the other numerals in Yorùbá. This is most obvious in the formation of numerals within the range of eleven to one hundred and ninety-nine. Numbers one to ten: okan, eji, eta, erin, arun, efa, eje, ejo, esan, and ewa serve as the core counting forms in the system. There are various types of reckoning for these numbers, they are done in slightly different tongues such as eni/ookan, eeji, eeta, eerin, aarun-un, eefa, eeje, eejo, eesan-an, and eewa. Beyond counting, Yorùbá numbers serve as numeral qualifiers and they come in the forms of ordinals and cardinals which are adjective forms in the language. There are also numbers that serve a larger than numeral role in Yorùbá mythology, religion and world view. Numbers such as seven, fifteen (also used in its reduced form eedogun), twenty (also known as okoo), forty and two hundred (also known as ogowa) are significant in various renditions about Yorùbá beliefs and lifestyle.

 

Derivation of numbers (0-1000)

The derivation of the other numerals follows varied patterns according to the range of numbers:

A.              First Pattern (11 – 14)

To derive eleven to fourteen, the suffix lá (which stands for ten) is added to the core counting forms 1 to 4. Thus 11, 12, 13, 14 are reckoned as ten plus one, plus two, plus three and plus four: Ookanla; eejila; eetala; and eerinla.

D.        Fourth Pattern (multiples of twenty and in-betweens)

Numerals in twentyÅfs start from twenty ogun to igba two hundred. In the course of counting, ogo and ogo, which are all variants of ogun, twenty is added to the core counting forms 2 to 9. Thus, we will have ogo+eji=ogoji which is forty; ogo+eta=ogota which is sixty; ogo+erin = ogorin which is eighty; ogo+arun= ogorun which is one hundred till we get to 180.

 

Between every two steps of twenty (such as between 40 and 60) in the system, twenty is divided into two to derive aado. Aado, which can be said to be Åetwenty less by tenÅf is then added to the suffix of the core counting forms 3 to 9. Thus, we will have fifty as aadota or 20 multiplied by 3 less by 10; seventy as aadorin or 20 multiplied by 4 less by 10 and ninety as aadorun or 20 multiplied by 5 less by 10. This is how similar numbers 90, 110, 130, 150, 170 and 190 are also derived.  

 

E.        Fifth Pattern (multiples of two hundred and in-betweens)

A similar system is present in the derivation of two hundred and its multiples. Multiples of two hundred start from four hundred irinwo to egbewa two thousand. In the course of counting, egbe, a variant of igba, two hundred is added to the core counting forms 2 to 10. It should be noted that irinwo four hundred is one of the numbers that are not derived in the numeral system. Thus, we will have egbe+etai=egbeta which is six hundred; egbe+erin=egberin which is eight hundred; egbe+arun=egberun which is one thousand till we get to 1800. Unlike two hundred ogowa best known as igba, two thousand is known as egbewa.

 

Similarly, between every two steps of two hundred (such as between 400 and 600) in the system, two hundred is divided into two to derive eedegbe. Eedegbe, which can be said to be Åetwo hundred less by a hundredÅf is then added to the suffix of the core counting forms 3 to 10. Thus, we will have five hundred as eedegbeta or 200 multiplied by 3 less by 100; seven hundred as eedegberin or 200 multiplied by 4 less by 100 and nine hundred as eedegberun or 200 multiplied by 5 less by 100. This is how similar numbers 1100, 1300, 1500, 1700 and 1900 are also derived.

Counting days before and after today

 

A good illustration of the functional usage of Yorùbá numerals is when talking about days before and/or after today. In Yorùbá, when talking about previous days one must always remember to count today in order to get the correct Yorùbá day count. So after ana Åeyesterday,Åf when you say Ågtwo days agoÅh in English, you must add one more day (i.e. today) to that in order to arrive at the correct word for Ågtwo days agoÅh (ìj}ta from |ta which means ÅethreeÅf) in Yorùbá. This is why English translation always gives one day less than the Yorùbá equivalent: ijerin (three days ago); ijarun (four days ago) etc.  Similarly, when talking about days in the future or days after today, one must always remember to also count today in order to get the correct Yorùbá day count. Therefore after ola (tomorrow) and otunla (day after tomorrow), when you say Åethree days timeÅf in English, you must add one more day (i.e. today) to that in order to arrive at the correct phrase; ojo merin oni (merin meaning four) in Yorùbá. Again, this is why English translation always gives one day less than the Yorùbá equivalent: ojo merin oni (three days times); ojo marun-un oni (four days ago) etc. 

 

 

Writing dates

 

In Yorùbá, writing dates also makes use of numbers. The numbers are put in positions according to their order in the month (e.g 1st, 2nd, 3rd etc). It also uses the either the traditional or modern method for the months. For example, vj< kcjz, oxù kcrin vdún is the 8th day of the 4th month, this is therefore the 8th of April because April is the 4th month of the year. However, for those who use the traditional naming system for the months; the date written above would be vj< kcjz, oxù Igbe.
