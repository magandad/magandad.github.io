Importance Haggling in the Yoruba commercial area

Unlike what we have in the departmental stores in the United States and most of the West, prices of goods are not fixed. Both the buyer and the seller haggle by the process of Dutch action. The sellers upon citing a prospective buyer beckon at him/her to her stall. The seller will briefly introduce the product to the buyer and ask for the quantity he/she wants to buy. By this time, the seller will give the buyer the price. This price, in most cases, is as much as 200% of the price that will be agreed to later. Both the buyer and the seller are usually skillful in the art of cutting down prices. The buyer who is not good at driving bargains is strongly advised to enlist the services of another because of the loss such an unskilled buyer is likely to incur. Male buyers are not perceived as keen hagglers as female, although there are exceptions to this. A hesitating or timid buyer may be accosted by two of more sellers at the same time. But once he steps before one of them, however prolong the haggling, the rival traders must make no attempt to invite him/her over to their stalls. This skill is part of what is considered essential to survival in the market and almost all commercial arenas.

 

Buying and selling in the local market

Local markets in Yoruba land can be categorized into two. First, we have those that are daily i.e., day-to-day buying and selling by the people in the town or village. It is often situated in the center of the town. We also have the second type, which is periodic. It is held at an interval of four to nine days. It lasts for several hours from very early in the morning till evening. While the former is meant primarily for the town’s people, the latter is patronized by people from different towns and villages, far and near. Products from these markets are far cheaper than in the daily markets. The location of the market that is periodic is usually in the outskirt of the town along the highway that links several towns and villages. The periodic market is usually highly populated whenever it is in session. In these local markets, whether daily or periodic, traders erect shed or hut as shelter from both the sun and the rain, others may just expose their wares and products in the open. Traders are departmentalized on the basis of the line of goods and products they sell. This means that you are most likely to find sellers of yam in one area; likewise fish sellers; pepper seller; meat sellers; and so on. This, we assume, has a lot of advantages. First, it makes it convenient for buyers to choose the best from so many varieties available in one place. It also gives the buyers a clear direction rather than having to perambulate around the entire market before getting what is needed. It also makes it difficult for the sellers to under cut the buyers. However, the sellers of the same product can form a cartel and this may frustrate a buyer. A lot of social negotiation is required of both the seller and buyer. The sellers are organized into associations based on the wares they sell. They regulate and look after the welfare of their members. They also settle disputes between members and between members and buyers. The Iyaloja (female marker leader) or Babaloja (male market leader) heads the local market. He or she represents the entire market in the various traditional systems of government.

 

Buying and selling food

In the case of buying and selling various types of food and foodstuffs, there two sides to it. In the first instance, there are those who sell raw food stuffs like beans, yam and yam flour, cassava flour, yam tubers of various types, rice beans and so on. The sellers either buy in bags and sell as wholesalers or sell in big bowls. The sellers have various smaller bowls for measuring the various quantities the buyer wants. Both the buyer and the seller engage in haggling through the process of Dutch auction too. In the second instance, there are those who sell prepared and cooked food. They are called iya olounje. The size of the stall that the iya olounje uses depends on the capital invested in it and the volume of customers expected to patronize her. Prices of food items are generally fixed but there are provisions for the customer to request a particular amount of a type of food. These two forms of buying and selling food and foodstuffs are dominated by women although there are cases of men in the business too.

 

Buying and selling modern items

Modern items include among other things, personal items, clothing, and household materials. These are cases of sellers who have small wares; hence they hawk these wares, which they place on their heads and move from one place to another soliciting for customers. After the preliminaries of haggling of the prices, the buyer may pay fully or part of the price and request that the seller come some other time to collect the outstanding payment. For those who have big stalls with many wares to sell, their activities practically follow what we discuss above concerning buyers and sellers at a local market. In addition to the selling and buying of modern items, there are departmental stores that also sell modern items but which do not permit the buyer to haggle on the fixed and often labeled prices. These are not common among the Yoruba and are generally located in major cities and patronized by ‘sophisticants’ with the financial ability.
