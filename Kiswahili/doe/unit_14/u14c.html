Unit 14 Mapishi

Cooking can be viewed as a hobby, a job, or a recreational activity. In the African culture, it is often viewed as a responsibility and as a recreational activity. The video clips, the narratives, and the text in this unit reinforce that view. When cooking, women talk, tell each other stories, council each other, as well as teach younger females the art of cooking. The socialization aspect of cooking is important to the women of the house. The team work spirit reinforces unity in other aspects of their lives. It is also a way of assuring each other that as a team they can achieve more than when they act individually.

Many African women living abroad make use of this aspect of socialization. If, for example, there is a party or a gathering where food is served, women will automatically offer to help the host with the serving and washing up afterwards. While they are doing so, they talk to each other, tell stories, or gossips. Those who do not understand this culture often wonder why the women would suddenly move to the kitchen or another available space for a gender-based socialization. For both men and women, the need to have a single-gender grouping at a social gathering is a natural progression expected at one point or another during an event. A mother's role is very visible in the home. She oversees and manages all the activities of the home. She provides instruction and direction to her family. She complements the role taken and has control over the planning of meals and deciding appropriate meal times.

This unit brings to light the culture of foods, food preparations and family meal time. Cooking is as much a social event as the eating of the meal. For many families, there is a flexible, but stated meal time at which all members of the family are expected to be present. In general, families will serve a meal when most family members are present. The set up and the manner in which the meal is shared follows from specific family traditions and general cultural norms of the community.

In the case of the family from Zanzibar, the evening meal is light and it consists of different types of breads and fish. They drink tea made by boiling water, milk and tea leaves together. Their evening meal is light because their main meal is lunch.

Many families may have their main meal in late afternoon, when they return from work (between 3 p.m. and 4 p.m.). As such, they may have a cup of tea before they go to bed and a small snack. For those that do not get home from work until after six o'clock their dinner will be the main meal of the day and may consist of most of the foods seen on the video clip.

Needless to say, there are no strict rules or schedules. Families will do what is most convenient for them but family traditions tend to be the hallmark of each family and contribute to the rich culture surrounding food preparation and food sharing.
