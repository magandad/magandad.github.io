A.   Greetings 

There are two forms used in greetings, jambo and habari. The form jambo is used in opening greetings while habari is used in subsequent exchanges of greetings. The literal translation of jambo is matter, thing, affair. As it can be seen in section one of the dialogues, the form jambo is used with specific prefixes 

                             singular                       plural 

1st person                             sijambo                        hatujambo

2nd person                            hujambo                       hamjambo

3rd person                             hajambo                       hawajambo

 

The prefixes are also the negative forms of each of the persons. Thus when someone says hujambo, it literally means you have nothing the matter with you (i.e. you are fine). The response sijambo is an affirmation which literally means I have no news (I am fine).  

Habari literally means news.' Thus, habari gani means what news do you have (i.e. how are things). In section one, in the conversations, we have habari za hapa how are things here, habari za safari how was your trip. The form habari can be used when asking about people (habari za baba), about the home (habari za nyumbani), about classes (habari za masomo) and so forth.  

B. Imperatives

The imperative verb form is used in a command, a request or an appeal. It is always directed to the second person singular wewe you or the plural ninyi you all.' There are two types of imperatives, the simple direct command and the polite form of command.  

The simple direct command is one in which the verb stem alone is used when addressing one person, or a plural ending is added when addressing more than one. Both types are used in the dialogues.

kaa  (you) sit                         kaeni  (you all) sit

njoo  (you) come                    njooni  (you all) come

karibu  (you) welcome                    karibuni (you all) welcome  

When these are used, the relationship between the speaker and the hearer is considered less formal and often requires the polite form tafadhali please to make it formal and polite. Alternatively the subjunctive form of the verb is used.  For example:

ukae  you sit   karibu ukae  welcome, (you) have a sit)

mkae  you all sit   karibuni mkae welcome (you all), have a sit 

The structure of the subjunctive verb consists of the second person subject pronoun (singular or plural), the verb root -ka - and the subjunctive form -e. Most speakers prefer the less formal imperative forms plus the polite form tafadhali. For example:

Karibu, kaa tafadhali instead of Karibu ukae 

C. The verb structure 

The verb in Kiswahili looks like one word but it has may pieces which constitute the grammatical structure of the verb. Lets take an example from the text. 

 

Mtoto                      a -na -sem -a

subject noun                 verb prefix- present tense -verb root -final vowel

child                       he/she  is  say/speak

The child is speaking 

 

The shape of the verb prefix is determined by the class of the noun. Here the noun child belongs to class one singular nouns (humans and animals). The tense will change depending on the time of the action/process. We will learn the other tenses later.

For all Bantu words, the final vowel in declarative sentence is a. Some borrowed words can end in e i, or u. For example these words borrowed from Arabic: samehe forgive, fikiri think, shukuru give thanks. The final vowel is also called mood marker. As a mood marker it can also change to either i = negative (in the present tense only: for example, anasema he/she speaks=> hasemi => he/she does not speak) or e subjunctive (discussed above). This will be brought up again later.

F.  Demonstrative pronouns

In this unit, three demonstratives are used: huyu, huko, and hapa. Others will be introduced as they appear in later units and lessons. The demonstrative huyu, which translates to this, can be used to refer to objects that are animate. Thus:

huyu ni mama.    this is a mother

huyu ni kaka yangu.  this is my brother

huyu ni mbwa    this is a dog

huyu ni mdudu    this is a bug 

When the demonstrative comes before the noun, the word order in the translation should  be:   

 this is + noun:  Huyu ni kaka yangu mdogo  this is my small (younger) brother

When the demonstrative follows the noun it should be: this +noun is:  ) Kaka huyu ni mdogo   this brother is small (young) . 

Both hapa and huko make reference to locations. While hapa makes reference to a location near the speaker, huko refers to a location away from the speaker. It can also mean a place near the hearer. In translations, hapa is here and huko is there. Thus: 

(i) Hapa ni nyumbani.   Here is home.

(ii) Huko ni mjini.  There (where I am not) is (in) the town.  

G.  The verbto be

In Swahili the verb to be is kuwa. But this is only used with the past and future tenses. The present tense form is ni which is used in the text and Mazungumzo 3.  

Examples:

a. Mimi ni baba.  I am (a/the) father.  

b. Wewe ni mama.  You are (a/the) mother.

c. Yeye ni mzee.  He is (an/the) old man.  

There are no definite and indefinite articles in Kiswahili. So baba may mean a/the father. It is often easy for Kiswahili learners to figure out, from context, the appropriate article for the English translations. 

H.  The Verb to have

There is no verb in Kiswahili meaning to have , to possess. Where, for example other languages express possession, Kiswahili uses a form that implies being with , being associated with.  In this unit it is used with the noun familia as in familia .ina watu watano. the family has five people. Below are additional examples of the verb to have used with present time.  

a. Mimi nina kaka mkubwa.  I have an older brother.  

b. Omar ana miaka mitano.  Omar has five years/is five years old.   

 I.   Nouns

One of the identifying characteristics of Bantu languages is a system of NOUN CLASSES. These classes have been designated in various ways.   Nouns and noun classes will be handled as they appear in the reading texts. For example, in this unit, we have nouns which refer to living beings and non-living beings. Nouns which refer to humans and animals fall into two groups. One group is characterized by the prefix m indicating the singular form and wa for the plural. Examples from the text for these nouns include:

m- (singular)                                         wa- (plural) 

mtoto      child                                       watoto   children

mzaliwa  native of                            wazaliwa  natives of

mzawa   citizen                                      wazawa  citizens

mvulana  boy                                        wavulana  boys

msichana  girl                                        wasichana  girls  

This group of nouns is also identified as class 1 & 2 nouns. Class 1 refers to the singular nouns and class 2, their plurals. The second group does not bear any distinguishing marker and context is used to disambiguate.  

no prefix  (singular)                             no prefix  (plural)

kaka   brother                                        kaka   brothers

dada   sister                                           dada   sisters

baba   father                                          baba   fathers

mama   mother                                       mama   mothers  

These nouns are often included in a class with nouns whose singular and plural forms are not distinguishable. The class is often referred to as class 9 & 10 nouns (to be discussed later). However, as it will become evident later, nouns like baba, mama, dada, and other nouns referring to animals behave like the nouns designated class 1 & 2 or m  wa and not like nouns in class 9 & 10 in which they are often listed in different grammars of Kiswahili might show. One noun which refers to living beings but remains in class 9 &10 is familia family.)  

J.   Agreement with Adjectives

The noun classes require a system of agreement.  This means that in a sentence, the noun must agree with all other words like the verb, adjective, possessives, prepositions, and demonstratives.   For example (underlined are the prefixes):

noun  -  adjective -  verb

mtoto     mzuri       anacheza A good child is playing

watoto   wazuri     wanacheza Good children are playing 

In this section we came across two adjective forms: dogo small and kubwa big/older. Let us pair them up with nouns in class 1 & 2

 (a) Singular

-dogo/ -kubwa   smaller/bigger or older

mtoto                  mdogo/mkubwa small child /big or older child

msichana mdogo/mkubwa small girl/big or older girl

baba                  mdogo/mkubwa younger uncle/older uncle (fathers brother)

kaka                 mdogo/mkubwa younger brother/older brother   

Notice that the adjective takes a m- prefix regardless of whether the noun has a prefix m-.or not. Thus the agreement prefix for adjectives associated with nouns in m - wa or class 1 & 2 or nouns that refer to humans or other living beings is m-. 

(b) Plural

-dogo/ -kubwa  

watoto  wadogo/ mkubwa small children/big or older children

wasichana wadogo/ mkubwa small girls/big or older girls

baba  wadogo/mkubwa younger uncles  older uncles (fathers brother)

kaka  wadogo/mkubwa younger brothers  older brothers   

With the noun familia family the pattern is as follows.

-dogo    -kubwa

familia  ndogo    kubwa

C. The Future tense and the Past tense

 

The future tense marker is ta:

 

a.  U-ta-kwenda wapi wakati wa kiangazi?

   You-will-go where time of summer

   Where will you go in the summer? 

b.  Utakaa  you will stay

c.  Nitazungumza I will speak  

 

The past tense is represented by -li- which, like the present na- or the future tense ta-, appears immediately after the subject marker. Sample examples from the reading text include:

Ulizaliwa mwaka gani?  when were you born?

Alizaliwa mwaka 1985  He was born in 1985.

E. The Subjunctive and the Prepositional verb

 

Review grammar notes in unit 1 on the imperatives for a revision on the subjunctive mood.

 

ukae  you sit   (karibu ukae  welcome, (you) have a sit)

mkae  you all sit

In this lesson we used: nipe  give me  (base verb pa give)

niandikie  write to me (base verb andika write) 

 

Note that in addition to the subjunctive form e- in the forms:  nipe and niandikie,

there is an applied suffix whose function is similar to that of a preposition in English.  In the case of niandikie the i- before the final e translates to the English preposition (deleted material) to. In the case of nipe the applied i- has combined with the final a to form e.

 

The shape of the applied suffix depends on the vowel in the verb root. A verb root is the part of the verb without the final vowel. For example andik-a. The preposition form for this verb is therefore i- because the verb root has the vowel i-. Other vowels that would condition the applied i- form are a- and u-.

 

Examples:

pata get = nipatie get for me

vuta pull = nivutie pull for/towards me 

andika write = niandikie write to me or for me

 

Supposing we have the verb soma read. The verb root has the vowel o. In that case the prepositional form will be e-. Thus: nisomee read to/for me Other verbs that would be affected similarly are those with the vowel e. For example: leta bring = niletee.  

 

Examples:

soma read = nisomee read for me

leta bring = niletee bring for me

 

Note that the prepositional form remains constant regardless of the mood (i.e. indicative negative, or subjunctive). The examples given here are all in the subjunctive mood.  We will come back to this and other prepositional forms in later units. Here are a few start-up examples with the verb in the indicative mood:

G.   The infinitive

 

The infinitive form is formed by adding the prefix ku- to a verb stem as in

ku-niandikia to write to me, kutumia to use kwenda (kuenda) to go 

kuandika to write

kunywa  to drink

kuzaliwa to be born

 

It can be used after a conjugated verb:

 

Ninapenda kusoma.  I like to read/study.

Ninataka kusoma.  I want to read.

Ninaweza kusoma. I can/am able to read.

Ninajisikia kula  I feel like eating (something).

 

The infinitive may also be used to translate as a gerund (verbal noun or the ing form of the nominalized and falls under Noun Class 15). This may be used as in: Kufundisha watoto ni kazi ya mama . Teaching children is a mothers job 


H.   Negative present tense

 

Mimi sifundishi Kiswahili I do not teach Kiswahili / I am not teaching Kiswahili

I.   The Habitual hu-tense 

 

The hu- tense is also called the habitual tense and it is used to express a habitual or recurrent action. The interpretation may be: generally, usually, or always. These have no specific reference to time. When this tense is used, the subject can be expressed as a noun or an independent pronoun and never as a dependent pronoun in the form of a subject prefix. Click for examples:  

Mimi hupika chamshakinywa   I usually cook breakfast.

Wewe hupika chamshakinywa  You usually cook breakfast.     

Yeye hupika chamshakinywa   He usually cooks breakfast.

 

Sisi hupika chamshakinywa   We usually cook breakfast

Ninyi hupika chamshakinywa   You all usually cook breakfast.

Wao hupika chamshakinywa   They make breakfast. 

 Although there is no time reference, the interpretation may be in view of the present tense. Thus, the negative of the hu- tense is the same as the negative of the present tense.

Note: Mimi hupika chamshakinywa => Mimi sipiki chamshakinywa

We saw unit 1 that the verb to be in the present is ni as in:

Ayubu and Mariamu were Hadijas parents. 

 

Future -ta- with -kuwa

Mimi nitakuwa mwalimu.  I will be a teacher.

Wewe utakuwa mwanafunzi.  You will be a student. 

Ayubu na Mariamu watakuwa wazazi wa Hadija.

Ayubu and Mariamu will be Hadijas parents.

This group of nouns is also identified as m-wa or class 1 & 2 nouns.  Class 1 refers to the singular nouns and class 2, their plurals. The second group below does not bear any distinguishing markers for singular or plural.  Speakers use context to disambiguate.

C. Personal Object Marker Pronouns

 

Personal object marker pronouns are also pronouns which may appear immediately after the tense marker and before the verb stem. These include: 

 

-ni-  me                                 -tu-   us

-ku- you                               -m/wa-  you all

-m-  him/her                        -wa-  them 

 

An example from the reading and video:  

 

Mama Omar anawaonyesha Omar na Abduli picha za familia.    

Omars mother is showing (them) Omar and Abduli pictures of the family. 

 

More Examples

1.  Ananipenda He loves/likes me

2. Anakupenda He loves/likes you

3. Anampenda He loves/likes him/her

4. Anatupenda He loves/likes us

5a. Anakupendeni He loves/likes you all (variety used in Zanzibar)

5b. Anawapendeni He loves/likes you all

6. Anawapenda He loves/likes them

Notice 5a & b. the final ni indicates plural.

 

The object marker pronoun is considered obligatory when the object is animate. This explains its usage in the example above. It functions as both an agreement marker and as a pronoun that may replace the full object noun in the sentence. For example: 

 

 Anawaonyesha (XXX) picha za familia.

 

She is showing them pictures of the family/family pictures. 

XXX indicates the omitted object that is also referenced by wa- them. In the video and text, the replaced object is Omar and Abduli  
 

D. The -me- tense

 

This is often referred to in some grammars as the perfect tense. The me -tense indicates an action that has been completed and has resulted in the present state of affairs. Consider the following example from the text.

 

Babu na wajukuu wake wamevaa kanzu nyeupe.

Grandpa and his grandchildren are wearing (dressed in) white robes. 

 

The act of putting on the white robes took place before the described present state (i.e. the act of grandpa and his grandchildren dressing in white robes took place earlier than the moment of speech). The me- tense is often found in descriptions of states of being. For example:

 

Mtoto amelala the child is (in a state of ) sleeping

Mtoto amesimama the child is (in a state of ) standing

Mtoto amekaa. the child is (in a state of) sitting.  

 

F. The Reciprocal Verb 

 

The reciprocal form of the verb expresses a concept of mutuality of interaction. The specific meaning depends on the verb with which it is used. The suffix -an- which is added to the verb translates as each other. We have examples in the text: -tembeleana, visit each other,

-amkiana greet each other.

 

G. The Preposition kwa 

 

There is no specific meaning for kwa. Its use is largely dependent on the context. The general meaning and functions are prepositional. Look at its use in the reading text in this unit.

Kuamkia kwa heshima. to greet with respect.

  

In some cases it can be used with a meaning that implies instrument and translates as by or with. It can also be used to mean on, at for and in as in:

 

For example:

 

a. Anakwenda kwa miguu.   She is going on foot.

c. Ameishi kwa miaka kumi..  He has lived for ten years.

B.  Locative Nouns

 

Locations can be formed by adding -ni to a noun. In this section we have one example: mezani on the table (meza + ni = table + suffix ni). Typical examples include:  

 

nyumbani                   at home

shuleni                    at school

maktabani                   at/in the library

hospitalini                   at/in the hospital

mjini        at/in town 

More <clickable>

When any noun is given a -ni suffix, it no longer belongs to its original class. Thus the grammatical role of the noun prefixes which identifies it with a specific noun-class is suppressed. The relevant grammatical marker is the suffix -ni. As such, the noun assumes a locative meaning and controls the agreement for the locative classes known as pa- ku- mu- or 16, 17&18 also known as the pa-, ku-, m(u)- classes. These prefixes identify the precise location in reference.  

pa- refers to a definite location or location known to both the speaker and the hearer.

ku- refers to an indefinite location or a location unknown to at least one of the interlocutors (generally the hearer).

mu- refers to a location inside. 

 

For example:

Nyumbani pazuri  good home (location)

Shuleni kuna wanafunzi at the school is good

Mfukoni mna pesa in the pocket there is money

A.       Use of  the Adverb baada after/afterwards 

 

This adverb is very common in everyday discourse. It is important to learn it early and to use it often so you can remember it. It is also notable that it is always followed by the preposition ya and a ku-verb. Thus we have: 

 

Baada ya kazi After work

Baada ya kusafisha mazingira hukaa kwenye baraza.

After cleaning the surroundings we sit on the porch. 

 

The opposite of baada is kabla before which behaves similarly:

Kabla ya kula anaoga. Before eating, he takes a bath. 

 

Another adverb that works similarly is badala instead.

 

Badala ya mwalimu. instead of the teacher

Badala ya kusafisha mazingira, tulisafisha madarasa.

Instead of cleaning the surroundings, we cleaned the classrooms. 

 

B.  The Question Word je: 

 

Je is an interjection type of question word. It can occur alone or suffixed to a verb. For example: 

 

Je, unajifunza Kiswahili? (so) Are you learning Kiswahili? 

Unasemaje boyfriend kwa Kiswahili? How do you say boyfriend in Kiswahili? 

 

From the lessons, we have these examples: 

Na wewe je? How about you? 

Mitihani ilikuwaje? How were your exams? 

 

C.   The Reflexive Marker ji

 

This reflexive marker translates to self. It is the same regardless of the noun type or person. For example: 

 

John anajipikia  John cooks for himself.

An example from the text: kujiandaa preparing one self. 

 

D.  The ki-Form: 

This is used to express a condition and may translate to if or when. For example:

 

A-ta-kuja a-ki-pata fedha

He/she-will-come he-if-get money

 

He/she will come if he get some money.

or

 

He will come when he gets money. 

An example from the unit:

 

-------(mara tu) tukifunga shule.

(immediately) when we (close school) end the school term.
