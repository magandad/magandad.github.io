A. Review of -po-, -ko-, -mo-

The pronouns -po-, -ko-, -mo- can be discussed as 'adverbial relative' markers. They have a functional interpretation of a verb 'to be in location' as well as a relativizer, when they appear as a pronoun in a verb or as a suffix attached to amba- 'that'.

These same form appear as relative particles of time. As such, they may translate as 'when', 'point of time', 'going on in time.' For example:
(i) Alipofika, alikula chamshakinywa.
'When he arrived, he ate breakfast.'
(ii) Nilikuwa nikisoma alipofika.
'I was reading when (point of time) he arrived.'
(iii) Aliniona nilipokuwa nikisoma.
'He saw me when (going on in time) I was reading.'

Needless to say, the same prefix would be used with the other forms of the verb ‘to be in location’, namely -po and -mo. Some grammars refer to these forms as 'adverbials' whose main function is to make reference to place/location. When used, we can get the following interpretations associated with the location prefixes -pa 'definite location', -ku 'indefinite location', -m(u) 'inside location.
chuo kipo hapa 'the college is (located) here'
chuo kiko Tanzania 'the college is (located) in Tanzania'
chuo kimo mjini 'the college is (located) inside the city'

C. Review of the causative verb

(i) Chuo kinawashirikisha wakulima vijijini (verb -shirika 'cooperation/ be involved').
'The University involves farmers from the villages.'

(ii) Elimu ya kujiendeleza (verb -endelea 'advance' from -enda 'go').
'Education for advancing one self (Development Studies).'

The causative form of the verb is used to express causation. There are different forms to indicate the causative. The Bantu suffix was probably y, which still exists in some words:

kuona 'to see' > kuonya 'to warn', 'cause to see'
kupona 'to recover (illness)' > kuponya 'to cure', 'cause to recover'

The y suffix has undergone sound changes in combination with various consonants, resulting in a variety of forms. The best advise to learners is to learn the two most popularly used forms, namely -sh/-ish or -esh- and -z/iz- or -ez-. The vowels in these forms depend on vowel harmony.

The examples in the reading text, shown here for convenience, have the -sh- form .

(1) ----- wanaomba kutowalisha wanyama.
'----they are requesting not to feed the animals.'

The base verb is -la 'eat' and the causative form is lisha 'cause to eat/feed.'

(2) Kanga hizi huonyesha utamaduni wa wenyeji wa sehemu hizi.
'These cloths showthe culture of the natives of these areas.'

The base verb is ona 'see' and the causative form is on-y-esh-a 'cause to see/show' Below are additional examples for instructional purposes.
simama 'stand' > simamisha 'cause to stand', 'cause to stop'
soma 'read/study' > somesha 'cause to learn', 'teach'
pika 'cook' > pikisha 'make/force to cook',
fika 'arrive' > fikisha 'cause to arrive', 'deliver'
zaa 'reproduce' > zalisha 'cause/help to reproduce'
penda 'like/love' > pendeza 'become attractive/likable/lovable'
toka 'leave' > tokeza 'emerge', 'come forth'

There are simple rules to remember:
(a) Most verbs-root ending with -k take the sh form.
amka 'wake up' > amsha 'cause to wake up'
kauka 'dry up' > kausha 'cause to dry up'

(b) Non-Bantu verbs take the -sh- form.
fikiri 'think' > fikirisha 'cause to think'
fahamu 'understand/know' > fahamisha 'cause to understand/know'

(c) Adjectives and adverbs take the -ish- form.
safi 'clean' > safisha 'make clean'
haraka 'quickly' > harakisha 'cause to hurry'

There are some exceptions which must be learnt as vocabulary items. Also, caution should be exercised when translations are made because the interpretation of causation in Swahili does not always match that of English on a one to one basis. In Swahili, causation may be used to cover such concepts as persuasion, permission, compulsion, and the general simple idea of cause. For example:


persuasion: bembeleza 'give/cause comfort'
permission: kopesha 'lend (assisted to have)'
compulsion: kimbiza 'chase ('cause to run)'

D. Combination of extensions
A Combination of these forms is also possible. For example:
(i) causative + passive = shw, (kope-sh-w-a ' be loaned')
(ii) causative + applied + passive = shew (kope-sh-e-w-a be loaned for)
(iii) causative + passive = zw (bembele-z-w-a ' be consoled)
(iv) causative + applied + passive + zew (bembele-z-e-w-a 'be consoled by/for/with)

E. Review the habitual tense -hu

This is used to express a habitual or recurrent action. The interpretation may be: 'generally', 'usually', or 'always.' These have no specific reference to time. When this tense is used, the subject can be expressed as a noun or an independent pronoun and never as a dependent pronoun in the form of a subject prefix. Thus:

Mimi hupika chamshakinywa 'I usually cook breakfast.'
Wewe hupika chamshakinywa 'You usually cook breakfast.''
Aisha hupika chamshakinywa 'Aisha usually cooks breakfast.'
Yeye hupika chamshakinywa 'He usually cooks breakfast.'
Sisi hupika chamshakinywa 'We usually cook breakfast'
Ninyi hupika chamshakinywa 'You all usually cook breakfast.'
Watoto hupika chamshakinywa 'The children cook breakfast.'
Wao hupika chamshakinywa 'They make breakfast.'

Although there is no time reference, the interpretation may be in view of the present tense. Thus, the negative of the hu- tense is the same as the negative of the present tense. This is due to the fact that the present tense na can be used to express a habit.

Mimi sipiki chamshakinywa 'I usually do not cook breakfast.'
Wewe hupiki chamshakinywa 'You usually do notcook breakfast.'
Aisha hapiki chamshakinywa 'Aisha usually do not cooks breakfast.'
Yeye hapiki chamshakinywa 'He usually does not cooks breakfast.'
Sisi hatupiki chamshakinywa 'We usually do not cook breakfast'
Ninyi hampiki chamshakinywa 'You all usually do not cook breakfast.'
Watoto hawapiki chamshakinywa 'The children do not cook breakfast.'
Wao hawapiki chamshakinywa 'They do not make breakfast.'
