Unit 17 Utalii

Tourism is one of East Africa's economic assets. It draws thousands of visitors from all over the world every year. They visit many points of interest such as the wildlife parks, cultural museums, and local villages and communities. They are very much attracted to the local cultures and traditions that are exemplified through songs, dance, fashions, arts and crafts, ways of life, and cuisines. Most famous are historical sites including preserved ruins of ancient autocracies and foreign occupations.

Visitors can also enjoy the clean sand beaches along the coast, the tourist hotels and facilities, and shopping at both the local markets and shops. They quickly learn about the art of bargaining and how to search for rare souvenirs at the market, shops, and from petty vendors. There are two important cultural aspects to note. One, the term duka 'shop' has an extended meaning which implies 'variety store.' In a duka, one may find as few as three different types of goods or as many as twenty types in display. These may include: clothing, hair products, shoes, food items, perfume, deodorants, toiletries and decorations. This is generally more typical in rural or suburbia shops. In the city, many shops specialize in one or two products.

If you want to buy an item from the shop, you have to ask the vendor to show it to you. If you like it, you pay for it in cash. If the item is clothing, you must know the exact size because the item cannot be tried or returned for exchange or a refund. The only items that can be tried at the shop are shoes and hats.

Not all items of clothing are bought ready made. To make a dress, one can buy the material from a fabrics dealer. Fabrics can be bought from a shop or from an outdoor fabrics market. Although the price of an item may be displayed, it is not fixed. The seller and the buyer can bargain for the best price.

Mount Kilimanjaro is another point of interest. It attracts many people, old and young, men and women, who want to climb to its highest point, the Gillman's Point. Not only do they learn the local conditions associated with the mountain but also adapt to the culture, learn from their guides and porters about the diverse social and cultural values of the communities living around the mountain. The guides and porters also learn from the visitors. As such, these encounters are beneficial to the visitors and the local people, a perfect way of learning, informally, about each others cultures and traditions. Another avenue for visitors to learn about the local cultures is places of socialization.

Group socialization is an important aspect of the culture. Men always go together for a drink and or a social gathering after work. Rarely would such gatherings include women. Because children are a woman's primary responsibility, working women try to spend as much time at home as possible when not at work. Men gatherings serve the purpose of a venue for discussing politics, business and social issues and networking, which reinforce male bonding. Women have social gatherings at each other's home where they can socialize and attend to their family obligations. Occasionally women may go to a public social place with their spouses or friend(s). Unlike men, a woman rarely goes to a public or social place like a bar or a restaurant unaccompanied.

Visitors would also learn that when invited to a restaurant for a meal or to a bar for a drink, the culture is such that the invitee pays for the meal or drinks. Thus, if someone says, 'lets go to dinner' or 'let's do dinner', it means they are offering to take you out to dinner and pay for it and any drinks consumed. An invitation to go out to eat is considered equivalent to an invitation to somebody's home for a meal and socialization. The affect of venue does not change the etiquette or the presupposed host. Likewise, if a group decides to sit together for a drink, each member is expected to buy a round of drinks. This socialization requirement can be costly and can force one to drink more than they intend to. It may be difficult for one to opt to take only one drink in such a gathering. This social etiquette can also be found in the English culture (in England), the assumed source of this cultural etiquette in East Africa. Lastly, when invited to someone's house the guest is not expected to take anything (such as wine, food, flowers, etc.) with them. The host would think that the guest was worried he/she will not have enough to eat/drink. Thus, some of the western etiquettes might appear offensive to a host in this and many other African cultures.
