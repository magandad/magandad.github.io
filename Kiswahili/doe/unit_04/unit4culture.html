A.  Times of Day

B.  The Concept of Time

A.  Times of Day

The day is divided into different segments based on the movements of the sun.  

asubuhi                   ‘morning’  (between dawn and noon)

mchana                  ‘afternoon’  (between noon and three o’clock, also known as adhuhuri)

alasiri/jioni             ‘late afternoon’ (after three and before six o’clock)

magharibi               ‘sunset’   (6/7 p.m., depends on the time of the year)

usiku                       ‘night’  (usiku wa manane, generally  between midnight and dawn)

alfajiri                     ‘dawn’   (5/6 a.m., depends on the time of the year) 

B.  The Concept of Time

The concept of time is understood differently from the west. Speakers use natural instincts to determine the time of day and often times rely on the solar movement. For example, the rising sun mark the morning hours while the overhead position of the sun marks the beginning of the afternoon. Evening is marked by sunset. In some cases too, speakers use the shadow (any type including human) to determine the time of the day. The middle of the day is marked by the absence of a shadow (because the sun is overhead). Also, one knows the end of the day is nearing by the length of the shadow.  

In telling time we use the same numerical number1 through 12 for hours and up to 59 for minutes. Telling time in Kiswahili begins with the first hour of daylight to the twelfth hour. Thus, speakers would use the following terms in telling time: 

Twelve hours of daytime

saa moja asubuhi     7:00 a.m.

saa mbili asubuhi     8:00 a.m.

saa tatu asubuhi     9:00 a.m.

saa nne asubuhi     10:00 a.m.

saa tano asubuhi     11:00 a.m.

saa sita mchana     12:00 a.m.

saa saba mchana     1:00 p.m.

saa nane mchana     2:00 p.m.

saa tisa adhuhuri     3:00 p.m.

saa kumi alasiri     4:00 p.m.

saa kumi na moja alasiri   5:00 p.m.

saa kumi na mbili jioni/alasiri  6:00 p.m.

Twelve hours of night 

saa moja jioni/usiku   7:00 p.m.

saa mbili usiku   8:00 p.m.

saa tatu usiku    9:00 p.m.

saa nne usiku    10:00 p.m.

saa tano usiku    11:00 p.m.

saa sita usiku    12:00 a.m.

saa saba usiku (wa manane)  1:00 a.m.

saa nane usiku (wa manane)  2:00 a.m.

saa tisa usiku (wa manane)  3:00 a.m.

saa kumi usiku/alfajiri   4:00 a.m.

saa kumi na moja alfajiri  5:00 a.m.

saa kumi na mbili alfajiri/asubuhi 6:00 a.m
 
Where we have shown alternate time of day or night indicates that the precise time of day reference varies depending on the season. Most people follow the movement of the sun to decide on the time of day.  

From the way we have displayed the time here, it is clear that it is very different from English. Midnight is not the beginning of a new day, it is the continuation of the same day. In other words, 12:00 a.m. in English time is the sixth hour of the night in Kiswahili. 

Many speakers of Kiswahili set their clocks and watches following the English system. However, they interpret it using the Swahili system. That is, if the hour arrow is showing 12:00 noon on a watch or clock, a native speaker will say saa sita mchana using number six instead of the English twelve. If it is showing 2:00 p.m., the speaker will say in Kiswahili saa nane mchana using number eight instead of the English two. This can be very confusing for non-native speakers of Kiswahili. Many rural farmers prefer to set their clocks and watches in the Kiswahili system to avoid this confusion. Non-native speakers are advised to do likewise. otherwise, a quick mathematical calculation can prove helpful. For hour numbers 1 through 6, add 6 and say the result in Kiswahili. For example, if your watch reads 2:00 (a.m./p.m.), you add 6 to 2 to get 8. Thus 2:00 a.m. in Kiswahili is saa nane usiku. 2:00 p.m. in Kiswahili is saa nane mchana. If your watch shows 8:00 (a.m./p.m.), you subtract 6 and the answer is 2. Thus, 8:00 a.m. will be saa mbili asubuhi, and 8:00 p.m., is saa mbili usiku. 

When expressing minutes, the Swahili system is not different from that of English. Speakers use ‘quarter’, and ‘half’ to express fifteen minutes after the hour and half an hour after the hour respectively. Thus 2:15 a.m. would be presented as saa nane na robo. 2:30 a.m. would be presented as saa nane na nusu. Speakers may also use an alternative option of telling time without using fractions. For 2:15 a.m., the speaker may also say saa nane na dakika kumi na tano’, and saa nane na dakika thelathini ‘for 2:30 a.m. Likewise, speakers have two options in presenting the time 2:45. One option is by using the actual minutes and say saa nane na dakika arobaini na tano ‘forty five minutes after two o’clock.’ The second option is to use fractions and say saa tisa kasoro robo ‘quarter to three.’ The term kasoro literally means ‘less than.' It is also often shortened to kasa and most speakers use the shortened form more than the full form. For instructional purposes, we will cite some examples of full hours and minutes. 

saa kumi na dakika tano alasiri   4:05 p.m.

saa moja na robo asubuhi    7:15 a.m.

saa nane na nusu mchana    2:30 p.m.

saa tisa kasa robo usiku    2:45 a.m.

saa tatu asubuhi na dakika kumi na tano asubuhi. 9:15 a.m.

saa tatu kasoro dakika kumi na tano usiku  8:45 p.m.

saa mbili kasoro dakika kumi asubuhi  7:50 a.m.
