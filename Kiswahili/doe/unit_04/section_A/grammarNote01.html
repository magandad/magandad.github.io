Grammar Note: Times of the Day

The day is divided into different segments based on the movements of the sun.

asubuhi ‘morning’ (between dawn and noon)
mchana ‘afternoon’ (between noon and three o’clock, also known as adhuhuri)
alasiri ‘late afternoon’ (after three and before six o’clock)
magharibi ‘sunset’ (6/7 p.m., depends on the time of the year)
jioni ‘evening/dusk’ (anytime after sunset and before 10 p.m.)
usiku ‘night’ (after 10 p.m.), also usiku wa manane, generally between midnight and dawn
alfajiri ‘dawn’ (5/6 a.m., depends on the time of the year)

Agreement

The ki-vi group of nouns is the most stable of all noun groups whose agreement remains constant across the board. That is, it reamains the same whether the agreement between a noun and an adjective, possessive pronoun, associative -a, or a verb. Thus:

kipindi kimoja vipindi vingi ‘one period/many periods’
kipindi kinaanza vipindi vinaanza ‘the period(s) is/are starting’
kipindi cha kwanza vipindi vya kesho ‘The first period/tomorrow’s periods
kipindi changu vipindi vyangu ‘period of mine/periods of mine’

Noun Group: Ø/N - Ø/N
Singular Plural
shughuli shughuli
wakati nyakati

Nouns in this group are characterized by their maintaining the same form for singular and plural. Ø here means no prefix is added to the noun to indicate singular or plural marking. It is important to point out that adjectives which take the same marking prefix as the nouns show a variation with whereby the singular takes Ø/N while the plural takes only N marking for adjectives. The nouns take a variety of agreement markers also when they combine with other parts of speech.

shughuli moja shughuli nyingi ‘one activity/many activities’
ratiba nzuri ratiba nzuri ‘a good program/good programs’
shughuli inaanza shughuli zinaanza ‘the activit(ies) is/are beginning’
shughuli yangu shughuli zangu ‘my activity/activities
shughuli ya kwanza shughuli za kesho ‘first activity/tomorrow’s activities’

Locative Nouns
Locations can be formed by adding -ni to a noun. In this section we have one example: mezani ‘on the table’ (meza + ni = table + suffix ni). Typical examples include:

nyumbani ‘at home’
shuleni ‘ ‘at school’
maktabani ‘at/in the library’
hospitalini ‘at/in the hospital’
mjini ‘at/in town’

When any noun is given a -ni suffix, it no longer belongs to its original class. Thus the grammatical role of the noun prefixes which identifies it with a specific noun-class is suppressed. The relevant grammatical marker is the suffix -ni. Thus, the noun assumes a locative meaning and controls the agreement for the locative classes 16, 17&18 also known as the pa-, ku-, m(u)- classes. These prefixes identify the precise location in reference. pa- refers to a definite location or location known to both the speaker and the hearer. ku- refers to an indefinite location or a location unknown to at least one of the interlocutors (generally the hearer). mu- refers to location inside.

The base form for the verb saidiana is saidia ‘help.’ Saidiana translates to ‘help each other, a reciprocal meaning.’ There is no consensus on the shape of the reciprocal form. Some scholars consider it to be -na, others prefer ­­-an- If we take the first suggestion, the structure of the verb would be saidia-na making it appear like the reciprocal -na was added onto the stem of the verb saidia. The second suggestion would imply that the structure of the verb as: saidi-an-a, indicating that the form is attached onto the root of the verb and may be followed by the final vowel of the verb. Many Bantuists prefer the later but the function of the reciprocal is not affected by either consideration of the reciprocal shape.

In many Bantu languages, the reciprocal -an- is considered a verb extension and it is used to express an activity involving plural subject. The meaning implies duality of the subjects in the activity performed. However, it is often translated as ‘each other.’ For example: Juma na Asha wanapendana. ‘Juma and Asha love each other.’ Baba Ayub na Mama Asha wanaamkiana. ‘Ayub’s father and Asha’s mother are greeting each other.’ Juma na Ayub wanapigana. ‘Juma and Ayub are fighting each other.’ The duality of the subject interpretation is apparent from the following examples excerpted from the reading text. Wanagawana kazi za nyumbani. ‘They divide home work between themselves.’ Walisaidiana kupika. ‘They assisted each other in the cooking.'
