Cultural Note
Until recently, birthrates were not an important aspect of ones life. The actual date of birth is not as important as the main event on the day one is born. In many cultures, one is named after the major event or experiences felt on he day they were born. For example, if one is born on a rainy day, they might be named as such and they name ould serve as a reminder to the individual as well as the community of that event or experience. Thus, birthrates and birthday celebration is a foreign concept necessitated by the need to document ones life for the purposes of services rendered by the civil service sectors of a government. It is, therefore, not uncommon to find an individual who does not know his or her birthrate and those of their loved ones like parents, siblings, and other relatives and friends. It is also uncommon for one to ask another for their birthrates. However, western
influence has brought birthday celebrations, which are now in competition with other major dates in one’s life, like weddings. Urban families have been the most affected by this foreign influence, spending to the tune of millions to celebrate a child’s birthday. Even the typical English ‘happy birthday’ song is an important element in these celebrations
