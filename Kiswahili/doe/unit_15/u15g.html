A. Review the a-tense.

------ awali naomba uniruhusu ---------nikushukuru sana
'----first of all, I ask that you permit me -----to thank you very much'

The -a- tense is considered a form of the present tense which is used in conversational Kiswahili.

Msemaji: Unaitwa nani? 'How are you known/called?'
Mwuzaji: Kwa jina naitwa Omari Hassan. 'By name I am called O. Hassan.'

The vender used the narrative-a- tense form instead of the regular present tense form -na. as in: Kwa jina ninaitwa Omari Hassan.

By comparing the two types, it is clear that the -a- tense has combined with the subject prefix to yield na- instead of the nina-. The same is true with other subject prefixes.

ni + na + itwa Hassan > naitwa Hassan 'I am called Hassan'
u + na + itwa Hassan > waitwa Hassan 'You are called Hassan'
a + na + itwa Hassan > aitwa Hassan 'He is called Hassan'
tu + na + itwa Hassan > twaitwa Hassan 'We are called Hassan'
m + na + itwa Hassan > mwaitwa Hassan 'You all are called Hassan'
wa + na + itwa Hassan > waitwa Hassan 'They are called Hassan'

Because there is no major meaning change between the use of the regular present tense and the -a- tense, the learner can make a choice in the use of the two. Needless to say, most speakers use the -na- tense more than the -a- in writing and occasionally the -a- form in conversation. There are no guiding rules for the use of either.

Due to their similarities, the -a- tense and the present tense -na- share the same negative structures. Thus:

(i) ninaitwa Hassan > Siitwi Hassan 'I am not called Hassan'

(ii) naitwa Hassan > Siitwi Hassan 'I am not called Hassan'
