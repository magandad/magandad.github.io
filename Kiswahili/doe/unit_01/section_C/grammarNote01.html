Grammar Note: The verb structure

The verb in Kiswahili looks like one word but it has may pieces which constitute the grammatical structure of the verb. Let's take an example from the text. (I thnk we should change to an example used in the text)

Kabuiya anatoka Kenya

Kabuiya a na tok a
subject noun verb prefix present tense verb root final vowel
Proper name he/she is come from
"Kabuiya comes from ....."

The shape of the verb prefix is determined by the class of the noun. Here the noun 'child' belongs to class one singular nouns (humans and animals). The tense will change depending on the time of the action/process. We will learn the other tenses later. For all Bantu words, the final vowel in declarative sentence is a. Some borrowed words can end in e i, or u. For example these words borrowed from Arabic: samehe forgive, fikiri think, shukuru give thanks. The final vowel is also called mood marker. As a mood marker it can also change to either i = negative (in the present tense only: for example, anasema he/she speaks=> hasemi => he/she does not speak) or e subjunctive (discussed above). This will be brought up again later.
