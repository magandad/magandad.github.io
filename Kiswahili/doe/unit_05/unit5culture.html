Unit 5

A.  Bargaining

 

In this language, the culture of buying and selling allows a wide latitude for bargaining. Venders tend to set their prices high to leverage a reasonable profit after the buyer has negotiated a reduction. For the most part, the initially stated price may come down by as much as fifty percent.  

There are some cases where bargaining is not part of the buying and selling culture. This includes services like restaurants, postal, book and magazines, medication, and drinks. In many of these cases the prices are set by the government after all necessary levy has been added to cover local and import taxes. Once goods are in the shops or markets, no additional taxes are assed on the part of the buyer. Thus, the seller may raise the price to cover the taxes he/she has paid to the government and to make a small profit. Needless to say, the vendor may offer a discount to his/her customer who has bought a number of items at once.  


The culture of bargaining offers a social aspect of the community that we need to consider. By bargaining, the vendor and buyer engage in a social dialogue which also brings out the art of negotiating. The culture looks up to negotiation as a valuable social code that allows both parties time to interact in a friendly manner. The buyer is seen as a guest who must be treated well and curiously while the buyer sees the vendor as a host in whose house he/she must remain well behaved. Anger, rudeness or impatience cannot be brought into the negotiation and at the end of the interaction, the vendor is happy for selling his/her goods while the buyer leaves believing that he/she had a good deal. In other words, all parties leave feeling like winners.
