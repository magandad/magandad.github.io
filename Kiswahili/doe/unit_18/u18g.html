A. Review of the verb to be -wa

In Swahili the verb ‘to be’ is kuwa. But this is only used with the past/future/habitual tenses. The present tense form is ni regardless of the person in reference. Thus:

Present

a. Mimi ni baba. 'I am (a/the) father.'
b. Yeye ni mama. 'she is (a/the) mother.'
c. Wao ni wazee. 'they are (a/the) old men.'

Past/future

a. Mimi nilikuwa/watakuwa baba. 'I was/will be (a/the) father.'
b. Yeye alikuwa/atakuwa mama. 'she was/will be (a/the) mother.'
c. Wao walikuwa/watakuwa wazee. 'they were/will be (a/the) old men.'

Habitual

a. Mimi huwa mvivu kama nimechoka 'I am usually lazy when I am tired.' b. Yeye huwa mvivu kama amechoka 'He is usually lazy when he is tired.'
c. Wao huwa wavivu kama wamechoka 'They are usually lazy when they are tired.'
B. The suffix -je

This form can be considered an interjection that is added to a verb as a suffix and that can transform a statement verb to a question verb. Depending on the context, it may translate either to 'what' or 'how'. For example:
(i) Unasemaje 'what are you saying?'
(ii) Unasemaje good 'How do you translate good?'
(iii) Unaonaje? 'how do you feel/see?'
(iv) Unaonaje kama tukienda mjini sasa?' 'what do you think about us going to town now?'

C. Use of idioms

In this section the idiom kula yako This is a very common expression that means 'sustenance, nourishment, or subsistence  (umepata kula yako was used 'you got your subsistence/nourishment).. In another context, it could also mean 'manner of'. For example: cheza yako = the way you play, tembea yako = the way you walk, sema yako = your mannerisms/ way of speaking. Note that any of the possessive pronouns (-angu, -ake, and their plurals) can be used with these items.

Expressions: moja kwa moja

This is an expression that is very confusing to non-native speakers. It does not mean 'one by one' mmoja kwa mmoja but has an idiomatic meaning of 'straight ahead' moja in this respect refers to one direction (i.e. only one way to the destination without twists or turns).
