Unit 18 Biashara

Open markets are a cultural tradition across Africa. The idea of an open market emphasizes the culture of the people. People go to the market not just to buy and sell, but to meet other people, to socialize, to exchange information, to debate political and social issues, to talk about their success, fears and hopes. The open market is a public forum at which voices of ordinary people are heard, where views are expressed and debated. Open markets are pools of information, institutions of both formal and informal education.

The location of a market contributes to our knowledge of the diverse cultures of the Africa people. At the fish market, we can see men, women and children from all walks of life, as buyers and as venders. However, most of the venders are men. At the Zanzibar market we see men only, both as customers and venders. The few women at the market are either visitors or foreigners. This is because in Zanzibar, it is the men who do most of the work outside of the home, including going to the market to sell or buy produce. At the Dar Es Salaam markets, we see a division of labor; the venders are mostly men while the customers are mostly women. This exemplifies life in the city which is very different from that found in rural areas. In the city, cultural barriers are broken to accommodate life styles and work schedules. At the village market, there is a gender mix in the buying and selling of goods. However, the village markets show both men and women as active participants in buying and selling. However, men buy and sell large items, especially non-farm products. Women sell food and home implements. This apparent gender distinction brings out the social, cultural, and tradition values espoused by the people.
