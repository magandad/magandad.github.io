Grammar Note:

The hu-tense
This is also known as the habitual tense. It expresses an action that is done as a habit and translations into English use the adverb ‘always’ to represent the habitual meaning.

An example from the text:

Sisi hukaa kwenye baraza ‘we usually sit by the porch/veranda.’

Sometimes the na- tense (present tense) is also used for the same meaning. However, it is good to use the hu-tense when the intention is to express an activity or action that is habitually performed.

Baada ‘after/afterwards’

This adverb is very common in everyday discourse. It is important to learn it early and to use it often so you can remember it. It is also notable that it is always followed by the preposition ya and a ku-verb. Thus we have:

Baada ya kazi ‘After work’
Baada ya kusafisha mazingira hukaa kwenye baraza.
‘After cleaning the surroundings we sit on the porch.

Another adverb that works similarly is badala ‘instead’.
Badala ya mwalimu. ‘instead of the teacher’
Badala ya kusaficha mazingira, tulisafisha madarasa.
‘Instead of cleaning the surroundings, we cleaned the classrooms.’
