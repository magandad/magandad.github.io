Grammar Note:

Review the -ki- form (in this unit lesson 4).

Another -ji form.

This one is added to verbs to make them noun that describe the manner of doing something, a skill, profession, or the art of doing something.

An example from the text: uchongaji from the verb -chonga 'curving'. By adding the -ji the verb becomes a noun that expresses the art or profession of curving. Someone who curves is called mchongaji.

Other examples of verbs and their derived nouns include:
-chimba 'dig' uchimbaji 'manner of digging'
-cheza 'play' uchezaji 'type of play'
-la 'eat' ulaji 'manner of eating'

Not all verbs take -ji to change to a noun. Some take -shi. Many of them are verbs whose root ends in a vowel or k-. For example:
-zua 'bring to light' uzushi 'fiction'
-pika 'cook' upishi 'art of cooking'

A note about -taka 'want', -penda 'like/love', -weza 'be able'.

They are usually followed by a ku-verb. In English, the ku- is referred to as the 'infinitizer'. In the text we have an example with -weza.
…wakimaliza shule wanaweza kuajiriwa na serikali….
'when/if they finish school, they can be employed by the government….'

Examples with -taka and -penda

Ninataka kusafiri kesho. 'I want to travel tomorrow.'
Ninapenda kula wali na nyama. 'I' would like to eat rice with meat.'
