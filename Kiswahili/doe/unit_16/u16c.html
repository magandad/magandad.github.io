Unit 16 Dini

The main cultural note for this unit is gender separation in the Swahili culture, a consequence of the Islamic religion to which many Swahili people are members. The best example of the gender separation is the accessibility to certain parts of the house of prayer. As the video shows, women are totally invisible in the mosque. The entire congregation here is made up of men. On this particular day of prayer, the women were assigned a place in another part of the mosque and listened to the prayer and sermon through speakers. They could not see or be seen by the prayer leader or the men in the mosque. This is a general practice and where the mosque does not have a separate room for the women, they may be allowed into the same location as the men, but must stay in the back a distance from the men. The preference is for the women to be out of the sight of the men while in pray.

From the Christian side, the video shows a mixture of western and local culture. The Sunday church service is modeled on traditions that came with Christianity. This includes the attire used by the celebrant and his aides. Except for the type of gifts and the manner in which gifts are given as an offering, the rituals for the Christian service follow from the western culture. The use of products, such as food and drinks, as offering during the church service, is an addition to the prototypical practice of giving money, a western concept of gift giving.
